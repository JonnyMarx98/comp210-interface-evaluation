Introduction

Kuthwala is a 3D puzzle game made in Unreal Engine 4

Kuthwala is the newest game from Toolbox Studios, featuring an expansive open world environment, with challenging puzzles dotted along the path to the final goal, the Temple! To add another layer of challenge and variety to our game, the player can swap between two different animals, each with unique abilities, which come in handy when tackling the plethora of puzzles scattered around the world!


In this I will evaluate the usability of Kuthwala's tutorial area which includes a few basic puzzles, particularly the control scheme, difficulty and intuativeness. I will do this using a qualitative interview, a quantitative based questionnaire and some observation.

Methods

Qualitative Data

To collect qualitative data I asked five people to play through the first 4 puzzles of Kuthwala.  After they had finished I interviewed them to obtain some detailed qualitative data. All 5 of the interviewees are computing for games students so this can also count as a heuristic evaluation. Below are the questions I used.

Quantitative Data

To collect quantitative data I asked 20 people to play the same few puzzles as the people who were interviewed. I also timed how long it took for the each of the 5 interviewees to complete the first 4 puzzles to provide some more quantitative data and find more correlations.

Observational  Data

To collect observational data, firstly I observed the 5 interviewees while they played through the first 4 puzzles and made some notes for each one. I also recorded the screen of 5 people who completed the questionnaire to use as observational data. I will watch and analyse these recordings and discuss any trends I notice in the discussion section.

Discussion

Qualitative Analysis

From the interview/heuristic evaluation quite a few problems were highlighted. One problem was that there was no tooltip for how to sprint, however only 2 participants mentioned this. The majority found the tooltips helpful but one participant who said they were helpful also suggested having something to indicate where the tooltips are . Another thing I noticed was that 4 out of 5 said that they found themselves using the panther more than the bear, mainly because it was much faster. This shows that there isn't a good balance between the two characters, slowing the panther could potenionally even them out a bit more.

Another notable finding was that players who had more experience with 3rd person games completed the puzzles much faster. For example particpant 4 and 5 said they play 3rd person games very often and they both completed the puzzles in less than 1:30, which is significantly faster than particpant 2 (3:30) who said they play 3rd person games pretty often. A potential problem is response bias, as the participants may feel pressure to give answers that are socially acceptable. This could either lead to them saying they play less than they actually do due to it being sterotyically anti social to play games a lot, or say they play more than they actually do as myself and the interviewees are games academy students, so they may beleive its more socially acceptable to play games more often.

After analysing the gameplay videos I noticed some interesting trends and common issues. One common thing was the lack of sprinting, most likely due to the fact that the sprint control is never told to the player. In addition to this the 3/5 of the participants that had their gameplay recorded do not play games very often at all. Another trend was the difficulty of the jumping puzzle in which the player has too jump across stones on top of water to reach the next puzzle. All 5 players failed this puzzle at least once, despite their previous gaming experience, this could be due to a few different things such as, the placement of the stones, the unrealistic "floaty" feel, or the lack of knowing how to sprint and therefore cannot sprint jump. One of the participants actually managed to pass 2 of the puzzles which meant that they did not learn how to pick up objects or knock over objects. This Is definitely a big problem as it means if the player doesn't read some of the tool tips or walks past them they can still complete the tutorial without learning how to play the game, which defeats the purpose of a tutorial.

Quantitative Analysis

Using the quantitative data collected from the questionnaire I found multiple interesting correlations. One clear correlation was between how challenging it was to complete the puzzles and how intuative the control scheme was. The particpants who found the control scheme very intuative found it very easy to complete the first few puzzles, this could be because they are more comfortable using the controls so they have more control over the characters in the game.

Another factor that affected how challenging players found the puzzles was age.  As shown in the graph below there is a clear correlation between how old players were and how challenging they found the puzzles. The older the player the easier they found the puzzles.

One question I inluded asked the players to rate how often they played 3rd person games to see if that affected the usability of the tutorial. There were two significant correlations I found, one was immersion and the other was how difficult they found it to grasp the controls. Below are the graphs showing these correlations, the players who played 3rd person games more often tended to feel more immersed in the game and also tended to find it easier to grasp the controls of the game.

Another question I included in the questionnaire asked the players which animal they found themselves using more often. As I developed this game and have tested it a lot in the past I expected the panther to be the more popular choice and I was definetly correct. 80% of the 20 participants who completed the questionnaire used the panther more than the bear. From my qualitative and observational analysis I believe thius is due to the panther being much faster and can double jump so players use it to get around the game world faster.

Conclusion

Overall the games tutorial was fairly well recieved by the particpants who tested it, the majority of the ones I observed looked like they were enjoying the game. Kuthwala has quite a good potential if the development was continued as currently there are a handful of usability issues as described in this study. The main changes that should be made are, the animal balance as the majority of players used the panther much more often than the bear, improving the way the tutorial is delivered as it is easy for players to miss a tool tip and not know what to do, improving the intuativeness of the control scheme which should also make it easier for players to grasp the controls of the game, and balance the difficulty of the puzzles as the jumping puzzle was failed significantly more than the other puzzles.

In conclusion Kuthwala has a lots of room for improvement, which is expected from a 1st year student game. The game just needed some extra time for the developers to polish and balance the game.
